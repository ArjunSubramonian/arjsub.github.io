Automated, Cost-Effective Optical System for Accelerated Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing (AST) Using Deep Learning
Calvin Brown, Derek Tseng, Paige M. K. Larkin, Susan Realegeno, Leanne Mortimer, Arjun Subramonian, Dino Di Carlo, Omai B. Garner, and Aydogan Ozcan PDF
ACS Photonics
We demonstrate an automated, cost-effective optical system that delivers early AST results, minimizing incubation time and eliminating human errors, while remaining compatible with standard phenotypic assay workflow. The system is composed of cost-effective components and eliminates the need for optomechanical scanning. A neural network processes the captured optical intensity information from an array of fiber optic cables to determine whether bacterial growth has occurred in each well of a 96-well microplate.

Selected Projects

Robust Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning for Binary Content Moderation Tasks in Natural Language Processing
Arjun Subramonian* and John Dang* PDF
COM SCI 269 (FATE in NLP)
Content moderation makes the Internet more welcoming. We investigate applying MAML to boost performance on binary content moderation tasks in low-resource contexts. We used the MAML algorithm, implemented in PyTorch, to pre-train a model whose internal representation is amenable to a variety of content moderation tasks with minimal finetuning. Our distribution of content moderation tasks comprised 8 tasks, including sentiment analysis and insincere question detection, each with a separate dataset. We compared the ability of this model pre-trained with MAML to adapt to perform well on unseen binary content moderation tasks to that of a model pre-trained using traditional transfer learning approaches and a model trained from scratch. Empirically, we found that MAML did not noticeably improve adaptation performance. However, we hope that, with additional improvements to MAML and fewer computational limits, MAML can be applied to train robust and adaptive large-scale content moderation systems in low-resource contexts. Our code can be found here.
On the Complexity and Convergence of Approximate Policy Iteration Schemes
Arjun Subramonian*, Shree Kesava Narayan Prasanna*, Nikil Roashan Selvam*, and Justin Yi* PDF Poster
EC ENGR 239AS (Reinforcement Learning)
Policy iteration (PI) is a popular method of converging upon an optimal policy in a Markov Decision Process. More specifically, we consider performing policy iteration with some bounded error at each step. This leads to the class of Approximate Policy Iteration Algorithms. In this project, we survey relevant literature in approximate policy iteration, and provide theoretical proof sketches involved in the analysis of the complexity bounds, convergence guarantees, and rates of convergence for various approximate policy iteration algorithms. We conclude with promising future directions of research in analyzing the performance of approximate policy iteration schemes.
Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning for a Policy Gradient Approach to MuJoCo Continuous Control Tasks
Arjun Subramonian* and John Dang* PDF
EC ENGR C147 (Neural Networks and Deep Learning)
Transferring knowledge from previous experiences to new, unseen tasks is a hallmark of human intelligence. Machine learning systems should exploit previous experiences to expedite learning. This has been accomplished in fields such as computer vision, where pre-training on a large dataset such as ImageNet can provide good initial weights for related downstream tasks. This method has struggled to achieve similar success in deep reinforcement learning (RL) tasks, where using it can even result in worse performance than training from scratch (i.e negative transfer). Hence, this paper investigates the adaptive power of Model Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML), a method that directly optimizes for fast adaptation to new tasks, on a policy gradient approach to MuJoCo continuous control tasks. While our experiments show mixed results for the efficacy of MAML on pre-training an adaptive policy network, with a more complex task distribution and fewer computational restrictions, MAML may have the potential to outperform the traditional pre-training approach in deep RL.
MovieLens Recommender System
Arjun Subramonian*, Amit Mondal*, Bryan Chiang*, and John Dang* PDF
COM SCI 145 (Data Mining)
Recommender systems are highly prevalent in e-commerce nowadays. For this project, we have a training set, constructed based on the MovieLens dataset, consisting of ~12M binary ratings consisting of user-movie-rating triples. For each movie, we have the title and genres. The goal of this project is to create a recommender system to predict the binary rating for ~4M unseen UserId-MovieId pairs in the testing set. There are generally two approaches to recommender systems: content-based and collaborative-based filtering. Content-based filtering recommends items to a user that are similar to items they have previously rated highly. In contrast, collaborative-based filtering operates on the assumption that users who have shared similar movie preferences in the past will continue to share common preferences into the future. We surveyed the performance of content-based (e.g. TF-IDF, genre-based decision tree, etc.) and collaborative filtering (e.g. SVM, SVD, element-wise matrix factorization, tabular matrix factorization, hybrid matrix factorization, etc.) methods on predicting binary ratings for each user for movies in the MovieLens testing dataset. We achieved the third highest ROC-AUC on the test set in our data mining class.
Quantum Programming Algorithms
Arjun Subramonian*, Vaishnavi Tipireddy, and Siddarth Chalasani Github PyQuil Qiskit IBMQX
COM SCI 239 (Quantum Programming)
We implemented Deutsch-Jozsa, Bernstein-Vazirani, Grover's algorithm, and Simon's algorithm using PyQuil and Qiskit. We then evaluated the implementations and modern quantum compile and runtime capabilities using the Rigetti and IBM quantum simulators and IBMQX quantum devices. We subsequently detailed our implementations, designs, and compile and runtime analyses in three in-depth reports.

Justice, Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion (JEDI) in AI

I am a fervent champion of justice, equity, diversity, and inclusion in AI research and beyond. I co-founded QWER Hacks, Major League Hacking's first-ever LGBTQIA event and the first collegiate LGBTQIA hackathon in the nation, aiming to increase the visibility of and celebrate the queer and trans community in STEM, as well as engage and bring together queer and trans individuals and allies. I also lead JEDI initiatives within ACM @ UCLA, employing actionable goalsetting and reflection to take concrete steps towards making the organization more inclusive of everyone. I created and produce the "You Belong in AI!" podcast, which inspires youth and college students of all identities and backgrounds, especially those who are underrepresented, to pursue AI opportunities

I strongly advocate to make an AI education acccessible to everyone. With the prevalence of AI in modern society and the harms it poses to already-marginalized communities, it is especially paramount that we empower individuals from these communities to have informed conversations about AI and fight against algorithmic injustices. As Outreach Director of ACM AI @ UCLA, I co-founded, lead, and teach an AI & ML course at North Hollywood High School (in Los Angeles) that emphasizes foundational concepts and fairness, ethics, accountability, and transparency in modern AI. Since my Outreach team and I unfortunately cannot teach at every school, we leverage educational technology to open-source our content in a fun, digestible manner; a prime example of this is through Teach LA @ ACM's online, interactive learning labs on topics like gradient descent and mean-squared error.
